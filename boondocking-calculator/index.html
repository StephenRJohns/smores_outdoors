Stretch it

    Estimates, not gospel. These numbers assume typical usage — your crew's habits will vary, so treat day one as a calibration day and watch your tank gauges. And whatever the math says: never dump grey or black water anywhere but a proper dump station.

    How the math works (every assumption, no hand-waving)
    1. Showers: people × showers/week ÷ 7 × minutes × flow (gpm). Every drop lands in the grey tank.
    2. Dishes: the flat daily figure for the method you picked (~2, ~4, or ~8 gal/day). All grey.
    3. Hand-washing & bathroom sink: a fixed 1 gal per person per day — our one baked-in assumption you can't edit. All grey.
    4. Toilet: standard and cassette figure ~0.4 gal per flush × ~4 flushes per person per day — RV toilets use short, user-controlled flushes, unlike residential toilets that dump a fixed 1.3+ gallons every time. That water is drawn from your fresh tank and lands in the black tank (or cassette). A composting toilet uses no water and makes no black water at all.
    5. Drinking + cooking: your gal/person/day figure. Most of it leaves via people; we count 25% to grey (pasta water, coffee dregs, rinse-outs).
    6. Fresh draw = all of the above added together. Every gallon of grey and black water started life in your fresh tank (plus any jugs) — that's why fresh drains faster than grey fills.
    7. Tanks start the trip fresh-full, grey/black-empty. No rain capture, no mid-trip refills beyond the jugs you entered.
    8. Days for each tank = capacity ÷ daily rate. The smallest number is your limiting tank and your trip length.

    FAQ

    Why does the grey tank fill up before the fresh tank runs empty?

    Two things gang up on you. First, on most rigs the grey tank is simply smaller than the fresh tank — a 40-gallon fresh / 35-gallon grey split is typical. Second, nearly every gallon you draw from fresh ends up in grey: showers, dishwashing, and every sink all drain there, and the only fresh water that escapes it is what you drink or cook with and what the toilet sends to the black tank. So grey fills at close to the rate fresh drains, but with less room to work with. Add a few jugs of extra fresh water and the gap widens even further — which is why experienced boondockers watch the grey tank, not the fresh one.

    What's a navy shower and how much water does it actually save?

    A navy shower is the classic water-rationing routine borrowed from ships: wet down, water off, soap up, rinse off. Even so, a typical RV navy shower runs about 4–6 gallons of actual flow — three minutes at 2.0 gpm, the defaults this calculator uses. Getting down to 2–3 gallons takes a shut-off-valve showerhead at 1.5 gpm with under two minutes of water actually running. Either way it beats the 15–30 gallons of a typical home shower, and in an RV every drop counts twice — once out of the fresh tank and once into the grey tank.

    Can I dump grey water on the ground?

    No — in nearly all jurisdictions dumping grey water on the ground is illegal, and it can carry substantial fines. Grey water isn't just water: the soap, grease, and food particles in it damage soil and waterways and attract wildlife to campsites. Many public lands, including plenty of national forest and BLM areas, explicitly prohibit it even where enforcement is rare. Always take grey and black water to a proper dump station — most campgrounds, many truck stops, and some rest areas have one, and apps and websites list thousands of free options.

    How do I make my fresh water last longer?

    Carry extra water in jugs — one 6- or 7-gallon jug is roughly an extra day for two careful people. Switch to paper plates and one-pot meals so there's almost nothing to wash, and use the two-basin method (one wash tub, one rinse tub) when you do wash up. Body wipes handle quick cleanups between real showers, and a jug in the shower catches the cold warm-up water so it can be reused for dishes or flushing. Small habits stack: most crews can cut their daily draw in half without feeling deprived.

    How accurate are RV tank sensors?

    Notoriously bad — they're the punchline of RV ownership. Most factory sensors are simple probes inside the tank, and a scrap of toilet paper or a film of grease across a probe will make a tank read full when it's half empty, or empty when it's nearly full. That's exactly why this calculator exists: a water budget based on your real habits is usually more trustworthy than the sender lights. Use the calculator for planning, then verify physically — look into the fresh tank with a flashlight, listen for gurgling drains, and note how the rig behaves as tanks fill. Tank treatments and a thorough flush can improve sensor readings, but never bet the last day of a trip on them.

    Do composting toilets really eliminate black water?

    Yes. A composting toilet uses no flush water at all: liquids go to a separate bottle you empty in any regular toilet or vault toilet, and solids go into a bin of peat moss or coconut coir that composts down and only needs emptying every few weeks. That removes the black tank from the math entirely and also eliminates roughly a gallon and a half per person per day that flushing draws from the fresh tank. The effect on boondocking range is dramatic — many rigs nearly double their trip length. Once black water is gone, the grey tank almost always becomes the tank that ends the trip, so pair the composting toilet with navy showers and basin dishwashing.
