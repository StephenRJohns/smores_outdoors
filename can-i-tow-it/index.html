Can I Tow It?

The trailer that "fits your tow rating" usually blows past your truck's payload first — this checks payload, tow rating, hitch, and GCWR all at once and tells you which limit actually binds.

1 · Your tow vehicle

Use your truck's numbers, not the brochure's. Payload varies hundreds of pounds between two identical-looking trucks — every option package eats into it. The first two fields are the must-haves; add the rest for a tighter answer.

How to find these numbers
  1. Payload — the yellow sticker. Open the driver's door. The yellow "Tire and Loading Information" sticker says "the combined weight of occupants and cargo should never exceed XXX lbs." That's your payload, as built, options and all. Not the brochure number.
  2. GVWR — the silver sticker. On the same door jamb there's a silver/white certification label listing GVWR (the most the loaded truck may weigh) plus front and rear axle ratings.
  3. Tow rating & GCWR — the manual. Your owner's manual or the manufacturer's downloadable towing guide lists max tow rating and GCWR by cab, bed length, engine, and axle ratio. Match your exact configuration — they vary wildly.
  4. Hitch ratings — the receiver stamp. The receiver under your bumper has its own stamped or stickered limits: max tongue weight and max trailer weight, usually one pair for weight-carrying and a higher pair for weight-distributing.
  5. Trailer weight — the trailer's VIN sticker. Use the trailer's GVWR from the sticker on its frame or tongue, not the brochure "dry weight" — nobody tows an empty trailer.

2 · What's in the truck

Everything and everyone riding in or on the truck counts against payload — before the trailer's tongue even touches the ball.

3 · The trailer

Use the trailer's GVWR (its own max loaded weight, on the trailer's VIN sticker) — not the brochure dry weight. If your loaded trailer fits at its GVWR, it fits on every trip.

Before you trust any of this

These are estimates, not gospel. Brochure tow ratings assume a stripped-down truck with a single 150-lb driver and nothing in the bed — a configuration no real camping trip has ever seen. The only numbers that settle it are real ones: load the truck and trailer the way you actually travel and weigh them at a CAT scale (most truck stops, a few dollars). FAQ

Why is payload almost always the limit before tow rating?

Because tongue weight lands in the truck, not behind it. A properly loaded travel trailer carries 10–15% of its weight on the tongue, and every pound of that counts against the payload number on the yellow door-jamb sticker. Add a family of four (400–600 lb), a bed full of camping gear (150–300 lb), and roughly 100 lb of weight-distribution hitch hardware, and a typical half-ton's 1,500–1,900 lb of payload is spent long before the trailer approaches an 11,000-lb brochure tow rating. An 8,000-lb trailer at 12.5% puts 1,000 lb on the tongue all by itself. That's why this calculator so often shows payload hitting red while the tow-rating bar still looks comfortable.

What's the difference between GVWR, GAWR, GCWR, and payload?

GVWR (Gross Vehicle Weight Rating) is the most your truck itself may weigh fully loaded — truck, people, cargo, and tongue weight combined. GAWR (Gross Axle Weight Rating) is the same idea per axle: separate front and rear limits that each axle must stay under on its own. GCWR (Gross Combined Weight Rating) is the ceiling for truck and trailer together, everything rolling down the road as one unit. Payload is simply GVWR minus your truck's actual curb weight — the budget left over for occupants, cargo, and tongue weight. You can be under GVWR overall and still exceed the rear GAWR, which is why weighing each axle separately at a truck scale is worth the few dollars.

Where do I find my truck's real payload?

Open the driver's door and look for the yellow “Tire and Loading Information” sticker on the door jamb or pillar. The line reading “the combined weight of occupants and cargo should never exceed XXX lbs” is your payload — measured for your specific truck as it left the factory, with every option package, trim upgrade, and factory accessory already subtracted. It's routinely hundreds of pounds lower than the brochure or website figure for the same model, because published numbers describe a stripped-down configuration. Anything added after the factory — a toolbox, bed cap, lift kit, heavy bumper — reduces the real number further. Use the sticker, not the brochure.

How do I measure actual tongue weight?

The simplest tool is a dedicated tongue weight scale: set it under the coupler at normal ride height and read the number directly. No scale? Use a CAT scale at a truck stop — weigh the truck alone, then weigh it again hitched to the trailer with the trailer's own axles off the platform and any weight-distribution bars unhooked; the increase in the truck's weight is your tongue weight. For small trailers, a bathroom scale works up to its limit, or you can extend it with the classic lever trick: rest the coupler partway along a beam that spans between the scale and a fixed pivot, so the scale reads a known fraction of the true load. Whatever method you use, measure with the trailer loaded the way you actually camp — tongue weight shifts with how you pack.

Does a weight-distribution hitch increase what I can tow?

No. A weight-distribution hitch doesn't raise your payload, tow rating, GVWR, or GCWR by a single pound. What it does is use spring bars to lever part of the tongue load off the truck's rear axle and back onto the front axle and the trailer's axles, restoring steering grip, braking balance, and headlight aim. It actually consumes payload — the hardware itself weighs roughly 100 lb and rides on the receiver, which is why this calculator has a checkbox for it. Many trucks require one above a threshold, commonly around 5,000–6,000 lb of trailer or 500–600 lb of tongue weight, and some receivers only publish their higher rating when used with weight distribution. Check your owner's manual and the receiver's sticker.

What happens if I tow over my ratings?

The truck will usually still pull it, which is exactly the trap. Overloading stretches braking distances, dulls steering response, and invites sway — the margins that decide whether an emergency lane change or a downhill grade ends well. It also accelerates wear on the transmission, brakes, rear axle, suspension, and tires; overloaded tires run hot and can fail outright. After an accident, operating over the manufacturer's ratings can be used to establish negligence, and insurers may scrutinize or contest a claim on that basis. Overweight-axle citations are also possible at inspections in some states. If a combination only works above a rating, the fix is a lighter trailer or a more capable truck — not optimism.
